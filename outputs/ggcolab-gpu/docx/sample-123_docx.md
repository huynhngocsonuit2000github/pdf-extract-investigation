# Protocol summary

## Synopsis
This is an interventional, single-centre, randomised, placebo-controlled, double-blind, multiple-dose study. A total of 80 study participants with type 2 diabetes (T2D) will be randomised in a 1:1:1:1 ratio to QW NNC04870-111 20 mg, 5 mg, 1.25 mg or placebo, respectively, stratified according to sex (male/female) and HbA1c at screening (<7.0% / ≥7.0%).

## Rationale:
The purpose of this clinical pharmacology study is to investigate the mechanism of action of NNC0487-XXXX on insulin sensitivity and pancreatic endocrine function in participants with T2D. Placebo is included to enable characterisation of the mechanism of action induced by the active treatment with NNC0487-XXXX. The study will compare the effect of once weekly s.c. administration of NNC0487-XXXX 20 mg, 5 mg, and 1.25 mg on the insulin sensitivity based on a hyperinsulinaemic euglycaemic clamp (HEC) after 40 weeks of treatment, including a 12-28-week dose escalation period. In addition, the study will investigate other parameters of pancreatic endocrine function and glucose metabolism derived from a hyperglycaemic clamp (HGC) and mixed meal tolerance test (MMTT) as well as measures of body weight, body composition, fat depots and bone parameters using magnetic resonance imaging (MRI), and biomarkers with relevance to mechanism of action.

## Objectives and endpoints
Overall design:
The study consists of:
an up to 52-day screening period.
a 4-day (3 nights) in-house visit with baseline assessments; body measurements, MMTT, HEC, and HGC being performed before first dosing (pre-treatment).
a 40-week treatment period (with dose escalation) on NNC0487-XXXX 20 mg, 5 mg, 1.25 mg or placebo.
end of treatment will include a 5-day (4 nights) in-house visit with MMTT, HEC and HGC performed.
a minimum 4-week safety follow-up period

## Study intervention groups and duration:
Participants with T2D will be randomised in a 1:1:1:1 ratio to QW NNC0487-XXXX 20 mg, 5 mg,
1.25 mg or placebo.
Treatment with NNC0487-XXXX and placebo will be initiated at 0.2 mg or placebo, followed by stepwise fixed dose escalation every 4 weeks. After reaching the target dose of NNC0487-XXXX 20 mg, 5 mg or 1.25 mg, this dose level will be maintained for up to 28 weeks, depending on dose. A total of 4 doses of investigational medicinal product (IMP) will be administered at each dosing step before escalating to a higher dose. During the study, participants will attend the site for 21 visits.
The study period includes 40 weeks of treatment with dose escalation in the first 12-28 weeks and dose maintenance in the last 12-28 weeks of treatment. Participants will visit the site approximately every 4 weeks for dose escalation in the dose escalation period.

## Number of participants:
A total of 80 participants will be randomised (20 to each of the three NNC0487-XXXX arms and 20 in the placebo arm).

Presence or history of cardiovascular disease including stable and unstable angina pectoris, myocardial infarction, transient ischaemic attack, stroke, cardiac decompensation, clinically significant arrhythmias or clinically significant conduction disorders within 180 days before screening.
Renal impairment with estimated eGFR <60.0 ml/min/1.73 m2 at screening.

# Introduction
Type 2 diabetes (T2D) is a metabolic disorder characterised by the presence of hyperglycaemia due to defective insulin secretion, insulin action or both. The loss of insulin sensitivity and insulin secretion capacity are key drivers of T2D progression. T2D is associated with cardiovascular complications and chronic hyperglycaemia.1
In 2021, the estimated worldwide diabetes prevalence was 537 million, and it is predicted to increase to 783 million by 2045. T2D accounts for over 90% of all cases.2
Approximately 80–90% of patients with T2D are living with excess body weight.3, 4 Weight loss is recommended in guidelines for T2D management, as it reduces fat accumulation in the liver and pancreas, leading to improved insulin sensitivity and decreased insulin demand, resulting in improved glycaemic control. Hence, weight loss in patients with T2D leads to reductions in HbA 5. Additionally, a weight loss of 15% or more can have a disease-modifying effect in people with T2D.6
The 2022 ADA/EASD consensus report proposes to equally consider cardiorenal risk, glycaemic and weight management goals when selecting treatment for T2D7. Medications addressing these important aspects of T2D management including prominent weight loss are therefore needed.
NNC0487-XXXX is a GLP-1 and amylin receptor agonist under development by Sponsor A to provide an efficacious and convenient QW s.c. treatment for obesity and T2D. The aim of the development programme of NNC0487-XXXX is to reduce body weight in adults living with overweight or obesity and improve glycaemic control in adults with T2D.

## Study rationale
The purpose of this clinical pharmacology study is to investigate the mechanism of action of NNC0487-XXXX on insulin sensitivity and pancreatic endocrine function in participants with T2D. Placebo is included to enable characterisation of the mechanism of action induced by the active treatment with NNC0487-XXXX. The study will compare the effect of once weekly s.c. administration of NNC0487-XXXX 20 mg, 5 mg, and 1.25 mg on the insulin sensitivity based on a hyperinsulinaemic euglycaemic clamp (HEC) after 40 weeks of treatment, including a 12-28-week dose escalation period. In addition, the study will investigate other parameters of pancreatic endocrine function and glucose metabolism derived from a hyperglycaemic clamp (HGC) and mixed meal tolerance test (MMTT) as well as biomarkers with relevance to mechanism of action.

## Background
Endogenous amylin is a neuroendocrine peptide hormone that is co-secreted with insulin from pancreatic beta-cells in response to food intake. Studies indicate that endogenous amylin is involved in the central regulation of food intake and body weight.8 Together with insulin and glucagon, amylin is an active pancreatic islet hormone that works to maintain glucose homeostasis.9
Glucagon-like-peptide-1 (GLP-1) is an incretin hormone released from intestinal L cells with a glucose dependent stimulatory effect on insulin and inhibitory effect on glucagon secretion from the pancreatic islets. GLP-1 is a physiological regulator of appetite, for which receptors are present in several areas of the brain.10
The potential benefits of combining different mechanisms of action to obtain more efficacious therapeutic treatment options with an acceptable safety profile for weight management and T2D are emerging.
NNC0487-XXXX is a unimolecular long-acting GLP-1 and amylin receptor agonist (RA) under development by Sponsor A to provide an efficacious and convenient QW (QW) s.c. treatment and once-daily oral treatment for weight management and T2D. By combining different mechanisms of action, NNC0487-XXXX is expected to provide more efficacious and safe therapeutic treatment options for T2D.
Within the NNC0487-XXXX programme, 2 clinical pharmacology studies (NN9487-4830 and NN9487-5022) and a phase 1b/2a study (NN9490-7613) have demonstrated a safety and tolerability profile consistent with previous findings for comparable GLP-1 and amylin RAs. In addition, the first-human dose (FHD) study, administering 2 x 50 mg NNC04870111 as an oral formulation (tablets) given once-daily (NN9487-4830), resulted in a mean change in body weight of -13.1% compared to 1.2% for placebo following 12 weeks of NNC0487-XXXX treatment.
The larger and more comprehensive phase 1b/2a study has furthermore shown a dose-dependent body weight loss after multiple QW s.c. dosing of NNC0487-XXXX with an estimated mean change in body weight of -22.0% after 36 weeks in participants receiving a maintenance dose of 20 mg NNC0487-XXXX versus +1.9% in participants receiving placebo, if all participants adhered to treatment.

## Scientific rationale for study design
The treatment duration of 40 weeks is considered adequate to ensure weight-loss-dependent effects on insulin sensitivity and pancreatic secretory function. Due to the length of the treatment
(40 weeks) and follow-up period (4 weeks), a parallel-group design has been selected.
A total of 80 participants will be randomised in a 1:1:1:1 ratio to NNC0487-XXXX 20 mg, 5 mg,
1.25 mg or placebo. Randomisation will be stratified by sex (male/female) and HbA1c at screening (<7.0% / ≥7.0%). The aim is for at least 60% of randomised participants to have an HbA1c ≥7.0% at screening. This will be monitored throughout the recruitment period, and the Sponsor may implement supportive strategies as needed to help maintain this threshold. Inclusion of the placebo arm will enable characterisation of the mechanism of action induced by NNC0487-XXXX.
Patients with overweight or obesity with/without T2D are the target population for the current clinical development programme for NNC0487-XXXX. Patients with T2D have dysfunctional glucose metabolism, thus, the impact of treatment with NNC0487-XXXX on insulin sensitivity and pancreatic endocrine function is most conveniently studied in the target population. Participants on treatment with metformin with or without sodium-glucose cotransporter-2 inhibitors (SGLT2)i are selected, as they are expected to be in the earlier stages of T2D with relatively preserved β-cell function, and hence appropriate for investigating the mechanism of action of NNC0487-XXXX.
Participants should be on stable metformin treatment during the study as this remains a foundational therapy in the management of T2D. The inclusion of participants with excess body weight (BMI 27.0 – 45.0 kg/m2) is due to the expected weight loss in response to 40 weeks of treatment with NNC0487-XXXX. A wide age range (18-75 years) is included to reflect the target population for the treatment, albeit with an upper age limit of 75 years to reduce the number of comorbidities in the study population.
MRI-based body composition assessment is included in this study to provide a comprehensive, non- invasive evaluation of treatment effects on fat distribution, lean mass preservation and bone parameters. MRI enables quantification of visceral and subcutaneous adipose tissue and muscle quality, as well as organ-specific fat content and bone characteristics, offering insights into the metabolic quality of weight loss. The inclusion of MRI supports mechanistic understanding of NNC0487-XXXX.
The HEC method is chosen because it is considered the gold standard for the evaluation of insulin sensitivity (M-value).13 During HEC, a constant insulin infusion rate (IIR) will be used to ensure
that the glucose infusion rate (GIR) at steady state conditions of euglycaemia (5.5 mmol/L or
100 mg/dL) is equal to glucose uptake in all tissues in the body (i.e. whole-body insulin sensitivity). The HGC will be used to evaluate β-cell function via an acute raise of blood glucose (BG) concentration to a predefined hyperglycaemic plateau (12 mmol/L or 216 mg/dL), where endogenous insulin secretion (first and second phase insulin secretion, via C-peptide assessment and de-convolution methodology) and other parameters of interest is measured. An arginine bolus will be given at the end of the HGC (120 min) to stimulate insulin secretion, enabling evaluation of insulin secretory response after a period of steady state hyperglycaemia. The MMTT will be used to investigate pancreatic hormone secretion and parameters related to postprandial metabolism mimicking normal physiological conditions after intake of a meal.
M-value is chosen as the primary endpoint, as it represents a measure of whole-body insulin sensitivity. Supportive secondary endpoints are chosen based on the current understanding of the physiological and pharmacological effects of amylin and GLP-1, to support the understanding of mechanism of action for NNC0487-XXXX.

## Justification for dose
It is well known that to mitigate gastrointestinal (GI) side effects with GLP-1 RA treatment, a gradual dose escalation to the target dose is required. Participants will be initiated at a QW dose of
0.2 mg and follow a fixed-dose escalation regimen, with dose increasement every 4 weeks, to target doses of 1.25 mg, 5 mg and 20 mg, reached after 12-28 weeks.
The starting dose, escalation regimen and maintenance doses of NNC0487-XXXX are selected based on the safety, tolerability, and pharmacokinetics (PK) observed in the phase 1b/2a study investigating the NNC0487-XXXX in a s.c. formulation (NN9490-7613). In study NN9490-7613, multiple ascending QW s.c. doses of NNC0487-XXXX were all deemed tolerable and safe up to 60 mg with the dose escalation being applied. As expected, a dose dependent increase in GI adverse events (AEs) was observed, as seen for other GLP-1 RAs, and has been shown for the long-acting amylin analogue cagrilintide alone and in combination with the GLP-1 RA semaglutide.
To further support GI tolerability in the current study, two lower doses (0.2 mg and 0.4 mg QW) have been introduced. These doses were not included in the previous NN9490-7613 study and are intended to enable a more gradual escalation, which is a well-established strategy for reducing GI side effects associated with GLP-1 RA therapy.
Further information about the expected exposure for NNC0487-XXXX in a s.c. formulation can be found in the current edition of the Investigator’s Brochure and any updates hereof.

## Lifestyle considerations

## Meals and dietary restrictions
During the study, participants should follow their routine diet as part of their T2D management, under supervision and agreement with the investigator.
During in-house visits (V2 and V15), participants will be served meals at standard times (breakfast, lunch, and dinner), and snacks, during periods where fasting is not required.

On the night before V2D-2 and V15D2, participants should have a snack and standardised dinner provided at the site. After this, they must fast from approximately 22:00. On testing day (V2D-2 and V15D2), participants will have a standardised breakfast and must afterwards fast for up to 6 hours during the MMTT. After 2 hours of starting the MMTT, participants can drink water. For more information, please refer to Section 8.2.4.
On the night before V2D-1 and V15D3, participants should have the standardised dinner provided at the site and start fasting from approximately 23:00. They should remain fasting overnight, during the HEC and HGC procedures, and for at least 2 hours between the two tests. This period may last for up to 18 hours until the afternoon of V2D-1 and V15D3. Participants are allowed to drink water during this entire period. For more information, please refer to Section 8.2.4.
Participants should be fasting for 8 hours prior to laboratory assessments requiring fasting on V11 and V19 (refer to Section 1.2). Participants are allowed to drink water during the fasting periods.
For restrictions related to prescription medicinal products and non-prescription drugs refer to Section 6.8.

Participants must avoid caffeine, tobacco, E-cigarettes or use of other nicotine products at least 30 minutes prior to measurements of vital signs.

## Screen failures
A screen failure occurs when a participant who consents to participate in the clinical study is not subsequently eligible for participation according to the inclusion/exclusion criteria. A minimal set of screen failure information is required to ensure transparent reporting of screen failure participants to meet requirements from regulatory authorities. Minimal information includes informed consent date, demography, screen failure details (end of study form), eligibility criteria, and laboratory safety samples. The end of study form must be completed, and the casebook signed off.
Screening and rescreening activities must be stopped if it is confirmed that an individual cannot participate in the study for any reason, to avoid unnecessary procedures being performed.

## Re-screening
Individuals who do not meet the criteria for participation in this study may have the failed eligibility criteria/parameter(s) repeated once (repeat procedure) or have all scheduled screening assessments repeated once (rescreening).
One repeat procedure or one rescreening is allowed in the following cases:
if the individual’s condition has changed (e.g., after recovering from an intercurrent illness) and the test result of one or more assessments with an ineligible test result is expected to change based on clinical judgement of the investigator.
if the individual failed one or more eligibility criteria due to practical circumstances (e.g., time since last blood draw, time since participation in a previous study, time since use of non- acceptable medication, or failure to fast as required).
if the individual failed one or more eligibility criteria due to technical issues for any laboratory parameter(s) (e.g., equipment failure, or sample is haemolysed or lost).
If elevated blood pressure at screening is suspected to be due to white coat effect, one repeat blood pressure assessment may be performed during the same visit or at a rescreening visit, at the investigator’s discretion.
Repeat procedure or rescreening is not allowed when the individual failed screening due to other cases than those listed above, e.g., due to laboratory results being outside of the allowed range, when there is no indication of a change in an underlying condition.
Individuals who have been deemed eligible for the study but cannot be randomised within the screening window may be rescreened. If the individual did not fail any eligibility criteria during the initial screening but fails one during rescreening, one repeat procedure is allowed in the same cases as listed above.
Individuals who are rescreened are required to sign a new informed consent form and will be provided with a new subject ID. For individual who are rescreened, the end-of-study form will be completed for the first participation, indicating the reason participation ended. At the rescreening visit, the previous subject ID (6-digit unique identifier) will be entered in the eCRF along with the new subject ID. All scheduled screening assessments, including laboratory assessments, will be performed in the rescreening visit. eCRF data from both the first and the second screening visits will be transferred to the study database, as applicable.
Individuals who fail a repeat procedure or fail rescreening are considered a screen failure and cannot have an additional repeat procedure performed or be rescreened again.

## Measures to minimise bias: Randomisation and blinding

## Randomisation
All randomised participants will be assigned a unique number (randomisation number) at the site. The randomisation number encodes the participant’s assignment to one of the study arms, according to the randomisation schedule generated before the study.
Sponsor A will provide blinded and unblinded randomisation lists. The unblinded study staff, who will not otherwise be involved in the clinical study procedures, will have access to the unblinded randomisation list.
Randomisation will be handled by qualified blinded study staff, using the blinded randomisation list.
Please refer to the TMM for further details about allocation of study interventions.
At screening, each participant will be assigned a unique 6-digit subject ID which will remain the same throughout the study (for rescreening, please refer to Section 5.4). The site will be assigned a 3-digit number (101) and all Subject IDs will start with the site number.

## Blinding
This is a double-blind study in which participants, care providers, investigators and outcome assessors are blinded to IMP allocation.
The active drugs are visually identical to placebo in the PDS290 pen-injector and will be available in identical packaging (see Table 6-1 and Table 6-2). Consequently, investigators, site staff, participants and sponsor will remain blinded throughout the study.
Sealed envelopes will be used for blind-breaking. Three sets of sealed codes containing investigational intervention information for each participant will be prepared. The sealed codes will be retained by the investigator (or representative), the local Sponsor A affiliate and the Sponsor A Global Safety department in a secured area.
If the investigator decides that unblinding is warranted, the investigator may, at the investigator’s discretion, contact the sponsor to discuss the situation prior to unblinding a participant’s IMP allocation, unless this could delay emergency treatment of the participant.
If a participant’s IMP allocation is unblinded, Sponsor A (Global Safety department) must be notified after breaking the blind, e.g., by reporting an SAE (see Appendix 3, Section 10.3 for reporting requirements). The date when and reason why the blind was broken must be recorded in the source documentation.
Once the study is complete, all hard copy codes (sealed and opened) must be accounted for at the site. The codes will be collected by the monitor at study closure visit. The laboratory responsible for PK analysis and the responsible development bioanalysis scientist in Sponsor A will have access to the randomisation list.

Participants with persistent and unacceptable hyperglycaemia should be offered rescue medicine,

## Clamp procedures

## Hyperinsulinaemic euglycaemic clamp
The HEC method has previously been used for assessment of insulin sensitivity13. A HEC procedure is performed at V2D-1 and V15D3, with the aim of assessing insulin sensitivity. Restrictions outlined in Section 5.3 must be followed prior to the HEC. The fasting restrictions before and during HEC are described in Section 5.3.1.
The HEC will be performed using an automated clamp system (ClampArt™) that continuously measures plasma glucose (PG) concentration, withdrawing approximately 2 mL of blood per hour, and controlling the variable glucose infusion to keep the PG concentration at a constant level18, 19. ClampArt™ automatically calculates the appropriate adjustments of the GIR using an algorithm based on the actual measured PG concentration and the grade of variability in the preceding 1 minute.
The participant will be connected to ClampArt™ at approximately 8:00 h. The participant will be fasting and in a supine or semi-supine position during the HEC procedure. The HEC glucose concentrations are maintained close to a predefined target in the normal range (5.5 mmol/L or 100 mg/dL) using a constant IIR, together with a variable GIR, to reach the steady-state function. The GIR reflects the pharmacodynamic (or glucodynamic) response to the insulin infusion. After the steady-state conditions are achieved, the parameters of interest are measured over a 30-minute period (refer to Table 1-4). The M-value (insulin sensitivity) is calculated from the parameters obtained during the steady-state period of the HEC.
For sampling of arterialised venous blood for ClampArt™ PG concentration measurements, a catheter will be inserted into a vein of a hand or forearm. This location will remain under a heating pad (about 55oC) throughout the clamp procedure. The heating results in an arterialisation of the venous blood, due to increased oxygen and glucose content to values comparable to arterial blood mediated by the vasodilator effect from heating. A low-dose heparin solution (100 U heparin/mL (10,000 U heparin/100 mL saline)) will be used to prevent blood clotting in the ClampArt™ double lumen catheter.
A second catheter will be inserted into a vein In the same arm for blood sampling. This cannula will be kept patent by use of a mandrin/stylet. A third catheter will be inserted into a vein of the contralateral arm for the infusion of insulin (50 IU of rapidly acting insulin (regular human insulin) in 48.5 mL isotonic saline and 1 mL of the participants’ own blood) and/or 20% glucose solution to maintain the defined HEC PG target level of 5.5 mmol/L (100 mg/dL). The GIR necessary to keep the PG concentration at the clamp target level will be adjusted and recorded every minute. PG measurements for safety glucose monitoring and for verification of ClampArt™ measurements will be performed at least every 30 minutes throughout the HEC clamp, using the Super GL Glucose Analyzer.
At nominal time 0 minutes, immediately after blood sampling, a primed infusion of rapidly acting insulin will be started with an infusion rate of 255.2 Mu*m2*min-1 which will be lowered from time 0 minutes to time 10 minutes to reach a constant infusion rate of 80 Mu*m2*min-1. This level will be maintained until completion of the HEC at time 180 minutes. Body surface area (BSA) is estimated using the Gehan and George formula20:
BSA = 0.0235 * Weight (kg)0.51456 * Height (cm)0.42246
Refer to Table 1-4 for timing of blood samples for pharmacodynamics.
Glucose concentrations ≤ 3.9 mmol/L (≤ 70 mg/dL) will not routinely be recorded as hypoglycaemic episodes during the HEC procedure. However, at the discretion of the investigator, decrease in glucose concentrations may be recorded as an AE of hypoglycaemia based on clinical concern or related to technical issues resulting in hypoglycaemia.
At the end of the steady-state period, the insulin infusion will be stopped. The ClampArt™ device will still infuse glucose at a variable rate to keep the participant’s PG concentrations close to the target level of 5.5 mmol/L (100 mg/dL), if necessary. The infusion will cease once the PG values increase above the target level.
Documentation for calibration/maintenance of the equipment used to perform the HEC assessments is required.
The time for early termination of clamp must be recorded in the eCRF.

## Hyperglycaemic clamp with arginine stimulation test
The HGC is a validated method in which the GIR ensures constant PG at a predetermined level (12 mmol/L or 216 mg/dL). During this procedure, the insulin secretion capacity and insulin
secretion at various levels of glucose exposure and after an arginine stimulation under hyperglycaemic conditions can be determined21, 22.
A hyperglycaemic clamp is performed at V2D-1 and V15D3, at least 2 hours after completion of the HEC procedure, with the aim of assessing insulin secretion and β-cell function. Participants must remain fasting during the HGC and the period in between the HEC and HGC (see Section 5.3.1).
The target glucose concentration will be 12 mmol/L. A primed glucose infusion of 200 mg/kg body weight will start at approximately 14:00 h (at least 120 minutes after the completion of the HEC). It will be followed by a variable infusion rate of 20% glucose, based on continuous PG measurement, withdrawing approximately 2 ml blood per hour, to maintain the glucose concentration at target.

# Statistical considerations
The statistical analysis plan (SAP) will be finalised prior to unblinding, and it will include a more technical and detailed description of the statistical analyses described in this section. This section is a summary of the planned statistical analyses of the most important endpoints.

## Statistical hypotheses
The following 1-sided hypothesis will be tested to compare 1.25 mg, 5 mg and 20 mg of NNC0487-XXXX each versus placebo for the primary endpoint: change from baseline in M-value in HEC (mg/min/kg). Let the treatment difference of the population means be defined as
μ = (NNC0487-XXXX minus placebo). Then the null and alternative hypotheses are given by:
𝐻0: 𝜇 = 0 mg/min/kg 𝑣𝑠 𝐻𝐴: 𝜇 < 0 mg/min/kg.
Operationally, the hypotheses will be evaluated by 2-sided tests with an alpha level of 5%.

## Multiplicity adjustment
A fixed sequence testing hierarchy is applied, where the hypothesis associated to the 20 mg dose level is tested before the hypothesis associated to the 5 mg and 1.25 mg dose levels. The hypothesis associated to the 1.25 mg dose level can only be tested following successful confirmation of the hypothesis associated to the 20 mg and 5 mg dose levels.

## Sample size determination
The sample size is based on the hypotheses stated in Section 9.1 of achieving superiority of NNC0487-XXXX versus placebo at each dose level in change from baseline in M-value from the HEC.
Based on results from Heise et al. 202233, a treatment difference of 3.5 and SD of 2.4 for 20 mg NNC0487-XXXX versus placebo is assumed. For the comparisons of 1.25 mg and 5 mg NNC0487-XXXX versus placebo, little evidence is available to support the power calculation.
However, it has been shown that 8-17% weight loss correlates to 64% improvement in insulin sensitivity over a 4-year period33. For simplicity, it is assumed that a 10% weight loss corresponds to a 50% improvement in M-value. In addition, modelling results show that a 5%-point difference in weight loss can be expected between 1.25 mg and 20 mg and similarly that a 3%-point difference can be expected between 5 mg and 20 mg. Based on this and linear extrapolation it will be performed, and it is assumed that a 5% weight loss corresponds to 25% improvement in M-value, or in other words that participants in the 1.25 mg arm will experience 80% of the effect of the 20 mg arm on the M-value. With similar arguments, participants in the 5 mg arm will experience 87% of the effect of the 20 mg arm on the M-value. Thus, treatment differences of 2.8 and 3.0 for the 1.25 mg and 5 mg arms, respectively, are assumed.
Under these assumptions, a total of 64 completers (16 in each of the three NNC0487-XXXX arms and 16 in the placebo arm) yields a marginal power of 97.9%, 92.8% and 89.1% for the 20 mg, 5 mg and 1.25 mg NNC0487-XXXX in comparison to placebo, respectively. By conservatively assuming independence between the three hypotheses a joint power of 80.9% is achieved.
Assuming a drop-out rate of approximately 20% in each arm, a total of 80 participants will be randomised (20 to each of the three NNC0487-XXXX arms and 20 in the placebo arm).

## Appendix 3: Adverse Events and Serious Adverse Events: Definitions and procedures for recording, evaluating, follow-up and reporting

## Definition of AE
An AE is any untoward medical occurrence in a clinical study participant that is temporally associated with the use of IMP, whether or not considered related to the IMP. An AE can therefore be any unfavourable and unintended sign (including an abnormal laboratory finding), symptom or disease (new or exacerbated) temporally associated with the use of an IMP.

## Events to be reported as AEs
In addition to events falling under the AE definition above, the following are examples of events that also are to be reported as AEs:
Any abnormal laboratory test results or safety assessments considered clinically significant in the medical and scientific judgment of the investigator, including events that have worsened from prior to the time point from which AEs are collected.
Conditions detected or diagnosed after IMP administration even though it may have been present prior to the time point from which AEs are collected.
Exacerbation/worsening of a chronic or intermittent condition including either an increase in frequency and/or intensity of the condition.
Signs, symptoms or the clinical sequelae of a suspected drug–drug interaction.
Signs, symptoms or the clinical sequelae of a suspected overdose of IMP regardless of intent.
Clinically significant medical complications occurring as a result of study procedures.
A ‘lack of efficacy’ or ‘failure of expected pharmacological action’ per se will not be reported as an AE or SAE. Such instances will be captured in the efficacy assessments. However, the signs, symptoms and/or clinical sequelae resulting from lack of efficacy will be reported as AE or SAE if they fulfil the definition.

## Events NOT to be reported as AEs:
Conditions present prior to the time point from which AEs are collected and anticipated day-to- day fluctuations of these conditions. This includes those conditions identified during screening or identified during other study procedures performed prior to visit V2D-1 (study day -1). Note: Conditions present or occurring prior to the time point from which AEs are collected should be recorded as concomitant illness/medical history.
Medical or surgical procedures (e.g., endoscopy, appendectomy). The condition that leads to the procedure is the AE.
Medical or surgical procedures not preceded by an AE or worsening of a known condition.

## Definition of an SAE
An SAE is an AE that fulfils at least one of the following criteria:

## Results in death
Is life-threatening
The term ‘life-threatening’ refers to an event in which the participant was at risk of death at the time of the event. It does not refer to an event which hypothetically might have caused death, if it were more severe.

## Requires inpatient hospitalisation or prolongation of existing hospitalisation
Hospitalisation signifies that the participant has been admitted at the hospital or emergency ward for observation and/or treatment that would not have been appropriate in the physician’s office or outpatient setting. Complications that occur during hospitalisation are AEs. If a complication prolongs hospitalisation or fulfils any other seriousness criteria, the event is serious. When in doubt as to whether ‘hospitalisation’ occurred or was necessary, the AE should be considered serious.
Hospitalisation for elective treatment (e.g., elective medical or surgical procedures) of a condition that was present prior to the time point from which AEs are collected and that did not worsen, is not considered an AE.
Note: Hospitalisations for administrative, study-related, social and convenience reasons do not constitute AEs and should therefore not be reported as AEs or SAEs. Hospital admissions for medical or surgical procedures, planned before study inclusion, are not considered AEs or SAEs.

## Results in persistent or significant disability/incapacity
The term ‘disability’ means a substantial disruption of a person’s ability to conduct normal life functions. This definition is not intended to include experience of relatively minor medical significance, such as uncomplicated headache, nausea, vomiting, diarrhoea, influenza, and accidental trauma (e.g., sprained ankle), that may interfere with or prevent everyday life functions but do not constitute a substantial disruption.

## Is a congenital anomaly/birth defect
Important medical event:
Medical or scientific judgment should be exercised by the investigator in deciding whether SAE reporting is appropriate in other situations. This includes important medical events that may not be immediately life-threatening or result in death or hospitalisation but may jeopardise the participant or may require medical or surgical intervention to prevent one of the other outcomes listed in the above definition. These events should usually be considered serious and reported as SAEs using the important medical event criterion.
The following must be reported as an SAE using the important medical event criterion if no other seriousness criteria are applicable:
Suspicion of transmission of infectious agents via IMP
Risk of liver injury defined as alanine aminotransferase (ALT) or aspartate aminotransferase (AST) >3x ULN and total bilirubin >2x ULN where no alternative aetiology exists (Hy’s law)

## Assessment of severity
The investigator will assess severity for each event reported during the study and assign it to one of the following categories:
Mild: A type of adverse event that is usually transient and may require only minimal treatment or therapeutic intervention. The event does not generally interfere with usual activities of daily living.
Moderate: A type of adverse event that is usually alleviated with additional specific therapeutic intervention. event interferes with usual activities of daily living, causing discomfort but poses no significant or permanent risk of harm to the research participant.
Severe: A type of adverse event that interrupts usual activities of daily living, or significantly affects clinical status, or may require intensive therapeutic intervention.
Note: An AE that is assessed as severe should not be confused with an SAE. Both AEs and SAEs can be assessed as severe.

## Assessment of causality
The investigator is obligated to assess the relationship between IMP and the occurrence of each AE/SAE. The investigator will use clinical judgment to determine the relationship.
Relationship between an AE/SAE and the relevant IMP should be assessed as:
Probable – Good reason and sufficient documentation to assume a causal relationship.
Possible – A causal relationship is conceivable and cannot be dismissed.
Unlikely – The event is most likely related to aetiology other than the IMP.
Alternative aetiology, such as underlying disease(s), concomitant medication and other risk factors, as well as the temporal relationship of the event to IMP administration, should be considered and investigated.

## Outcome of AEs
The investigator will select the most appropriate outcome that reflects the event outcome status at the time of reporting:
Recovered/resolved: The participant has fully recovered from the event, or by medical or surgical treatment the condition has returned to the level observed when first documented.
Recovering/resolving: The condition is improving, and the participant is expected to recover from the event. This term may also be applicable for AEs ongoing at the time of death (where death is due to another AE).
Note: SAEs can be closed with the outcome ‘recovering/resolving’ only if the participant has withdrawn or completed the study, or end of study is near, and the investigator confirms that the participant is expected to fully recover from the event.
Recovered/resolved with sequelae: The participant has recovered from the event but with long-lasting or permanent effect(s) due to a disease, injury, treatment or procedure. If a sequela meets an SAE criterion, the AE must be reported as an SAE.
Not recovered/not resolved: The condition has not improved. This term may be applicable in cases of chronic conditions, cancer or AEs ongoing at time of death (where death is due to another AE).
Fatal: This term is only applicable if the participant died from a condition related to the reported AE. Outcomes of other reported AEs that occurred before the participant died should be assessed as ‘recovered/resolved’, ‘recovering/resolving’, ‘recovered/resolved with sequelae’ or ‘not recovered/not resolved’. An AE with a fatal outcome must be reported as an SAE.
Unknown: The outcome is unknown.
Note: AEs can be closed with the outcome ‘unknown’ only if the participant is lost to follow-up.

Severe hypoglycaemia
aSevere hypoglycaemia is an event requiring assistance of another person to actively administer carbohydrates, glucagon, or take other corrective actions. Plasma glucose concentrations may not be available during an event, but neurological recovery following the return of plasma glucose to normal is considered sufficient evidence that the event was induced by a low plasma glucose concentration.47

## Nocturnal hypoglycaemia
Nocturnal hypoglycaemic episodes: episodes occurring between 00:01 and 05:59 both inclusive.

## Reporting of hypoglycaemic episodes
Reporting of hypoglycaemic episodes by BG meters:
PG should always be recorded in the diary when a hypoglycaemic episode is suspected.
When a participant experiences a hypoglycaemic episode, the participant should record the general information in relation to the hypoglycaemia (timing, BG measurements, symptoms, etc.) as described in the diary. The investigator should ensure correct reporting of the hypoglycaemic episode from the paper diary to the eCRF (hypoglycaemic episodes form).
If a participant is not able to fill in the diary at the time of the hypoglycaemic episode (e.g., in case of hospitalisation), the investigator should still report the episode in the hypoglycaemic episode form. The event should be reported as an SAE by the investigator, if applicable according to Appendix 3 (Section 10.3.3) by completing an AE form and a safety information form. One AE form and safety information form can cover several hypoglycaemic episode if the participant has not recovered between the episodes, see Appendix 3 (Section 10.3.3).
For reporting of hypoglycaemia in relation to HECs, see Section 8.2.3.1.
A FPG result <3.9 mmol/L (70 mg/dL) in relation to planned fasting visits should not be reported as a hypoglycaemic episode, but only as an AE at the discretion of the investigator (see Table 10-1 and Appendix 3, Section 10.3).
Upon onset of a hypoglycaemic episode the participant is recommended to measure BG every 15 minutes until the BG value is ≥3.9 mmol/L (70 mg/dL) and/or symptoms have been resolved in accordance with current guidelines.47
Repeated BG measurements and/or symptoms will by default be considered as one hypoglycaemic episode until a succeeding BG value is ≥ 3.9 mmol/L (70 mg/dL) and/or symptoms have been resolved and should be reported as only one hypoglycaemic episode. In case of several low BG values within the hypoglycaemic episode, the lowest value is the one that will be reported as the BG value for the hypoglycaemic episode, but the start time of the episode will remain as the time for the first low BG value and/or symptom. The remaining values will be kept as source data.
If the severity of a hypoglycaemic episode changes, only one hypoglycaemic episode will be reported, reflecting the most severe degree of hypoglycaemia.
Regarding the question “could you help yourself?”, the investigator must instruct the participants to answer “No. I needed help and would not have managed on my own”, if the episode was an event that required assistance of another person to actively administer carbohydrate, glucagon, or take other corrective actions due to significant cognitive impairment. BG concentrations may not be available during an event, but neurological recovery following the return of BG to normal is considered sufficient evidence that the event was induced by a low BG concentration.
